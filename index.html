“What I’m Interested in is Something that Violates the Law”
Regulatory Practitioner Views on Automated Detection of Deceptive Design Patterns

Arianna Rossi1, Simon Parkin2
1 LIDER-Lab, DIRPOLIS, Sant’Anna School of Advanced Studies, Pisa  ·  2 TU Delft
CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems ’26 — Barcelona
Paper DOI

Motivation

Despite growing regulatory oversight (OECD, 2022; EDPB, 2023), enforcement of deceptive design patterns (DPs) races to keep up with their scale online. Academia has invested significant research capital in automated detection tools — from crawlers to LLM-based classifiers — many of which claim utility for regulators without direct validation. This study examines whether the capacity and capabilities of regulatory practice can match the implied capacity of such tooling. We critically examine the disconnect between approaches that frame DP detection as a pattern-matching problem and regulatory practice, which demands legal reasoning and contextual sensitivity.

Key Results

1

Enforcement needs solid evidence of non-compliance against legal provisions — not research-centred catalogues of DPs.

2

Regulators choose which comparatively few cases they pursue from across the whole internet: a bottleneck that existing tools need to account for.

3

The entire process of gathering and analysing information for enforcement purposes must be traceable and auditable.

Existing Academic Tools

🔍

Crawlers & Rule-Based

Large-scale web scraping with fixed rules. Pioneered by Mathur et al. (2019) and Nouwens et al. (2020). Focus on e-commerce and cookies.

🤖

ML Pipelines

Computer vision + NLP classifiers that recognize DPs on annotated datasets of GUI screenshots, such as AidUI (Mansur et al., 2023) and UIGuard (Chen et al., 2023).

✨

LLM-Based

Multimodal flexibility beyond GUIs alone. Use proprietary models as baseline, such as DeceptiLens (Bäumler et al., 2025) and DPGuard (Shi et al., 2025).

Research Questions

RQ 1

In which activities do practitioners of the regulatory sphere employ computing technology?

RQ 2

What requirements must computing technologies have to be used in their work?

RQ 3

In which enforcement activities are AI-based tools developed by academia most useful, and what requirements do they embed?

Methodology

We interviewed nine practitioners across EU regulatory, policy-making, business, and civil society organizations involved in DP detection and EnfTech deployment (Jan–Apr 2025, 45–60 min each). Transcripts were analysed via codebook-style Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2021) by two independent coders.

RQ1: The Enforcement Pipeline

Detection and enforcement involve multiple, non-mandatory phases. Practitioners use tools to automate tasks beyond detection (e.g., contacting businesses, issuing reports), while some steps require human input (e.g., legal assessment, filing complaints).

1

Preventative Action

Official guidelines, self-assessment tools, consultancy services to help businesses adhere to regulations upfront.

“Avoid change management” — BUZ-P1
2

Sweep Collection

Large-scale sweeps of GUI-visible practices, manual or automated. Can involve scraping thousands of websites.

“We wanted to know if the suspicion could be proven” — CP-P9
3

Screening

Data analysis guided by priorities, complaints, media attention, or company size. Accuracy is secondary — tools provide indicators of possible violations.

“We cannot fine 30,000 businesses even in 100 years” — CP-P4
4

Further Information Gathering

Data beyond what is collected online: verifying stock messages, identifying service owners, conducting audits.

“Investigative software can only be used as a hunch” — PM-P2
5

Establish Violations

Matching dark patterns to legal violations. Complex and always requires human validation.

“Even the mentioning of a dark pattern doesn’t mean anything to me. In the end, what I’m interested in is something that violates the law” — CP-P4
6

Take Action

Complaints, fines, or remediation opportunities. Smaller businesses often get a chance to redress first.

“We communicate to traders and give them an opportunity to change” — CP-P4
7

Check Change Implementation

Post-enforcement monitoring to verify changes and detect reappearance of dark patterns over time.

“Maybe in one year they put back what they had before” — NGO-P3

RQ2: Requirements for Technology Adoption

Requirements most prominently mentioned by the interviewees to enable technology adoption:

🔍 Trustworthiness

Transparency, reliability, verifiability, traceability. Data must withstand judicial scrutiny.

🧑‍⚖️ Human Oversight

Non-negotiable. No participant believed legal processes could rely solely on automated tools.

💰 Cost & Capacity

Technologies help do more with less, but data overload without filtering hampers rather than helps.

🔓 Open Source

Essential for transparency, reproducibility, sharing across authorities, and cutting procurement burdens.

🔐 Confidentiality

Enforcement data must remain protected during and after investigations. Tools using third-party APIs (especially proprietary LLMs) raise serious concerns about data leakage and chain-of-custody integrity.

⏱️ Timeliness & Traceability

Tools must produce timestamped evidence showing when data was collected, from which source, and under what conditions — essential for admissibility in legal proceedings.

“It’s very hard for us to take machine learning models and LLM output at face value.” DP-P6

Additional Insights

💡 Tools as Inspiration

Detection tools inspire in-house development but are rarely expected in legal procedures directly.

🎯 Campaigns, Not Coverage

Investigations target specific DP families matching clear legal violations. Broad-coverage tools were considered too general.

📊 Scaling Diligence

AI-tools seen as scaling up to match DP prevalence — not augmenting regulator skills. Repetitive tasks are the sweet spot.

RQ3: Where Do Tools Fit in the Pipeline?

All analysed tools are useful for Sweep Collection (Step 2). Only cookie-banner-focused tools (Nouwens et al., 2020; Kirkman & Vines, 2023; Bouhoula et al., 2023) support Screening and Establishing Violations.

The fundamental mismatch. Academic tools treat enforcement as a pattern-matching problem. But regulators need a plausible legal narrative connecting design choices to specific legal violations, supported by evidence that can survive judicial scrutiny. Detection is only the beginning of a long, resource-constrained process.

Discussion & Recommendations

The results show that academic tools and enforcement practice operate under fundamentally different logics. Where researchers optimize for detection coverage and classification accuracy, regulators need legally grounded evidence that connects interface designs to specific legal violations. This mismatch is not merely technical: it reflects a deeper disconnect in how the problem is framed. Below, we offer five actionable directions to align supply and demand.

R1

Map Case Law to DPs & Co-Design Legal Technologies

The primary goal of regulators is justifying why a design pattern is a possible legal violation, not merely finding DPs. This requires jurisdiction-specific mappings of known violations to known dark patterns, building on work by Leiser & Santos (2023) and Brignull (2023). Outputs should be computable and interoperable via formal legal ontologies (Pandit et al., 2019; Palmirani et al., 2018), and leverage LLMs fine-tuned for legal reasoning rather than general-purpose models. The DP community — which has already combined HCI and law effectively (Gray et al., 2024; Santos & Rossi, 2023) — now needs to involve computer scientists expert in legal knowledge representation.

R2

Identify Enforcement Actors & Engage Early

Researchers must identify relevant stakeholders and model their diverse needs into user requirements. Tool functionalities must align with operational constraints — particularly institutional cost capacity and the slow complexities of rigid procurement procedures. Research tools often claim utility for regulators, but their actual use is constrained because they do not align with evidentiary needs and investigatory methods — a limitation also recently noted by Gunawan et al. (2025). The heterogeneity of institutional capacities must be considered: some agencies develop tools in-house, while others rely on legal experts with limited technical literacy. The EU AI Act’s Article 4 mandates AI literacy, creating opportunities for HCI researchers and digital educators.

R3

Design Beyond Detection

There are many enforcement tasks where academia can contribute beyond DP detection: web crawlers for timestamped evidence collection, systems for organizing and prioritizing evidence, monitoring changes over time, and helping manage complaints. Research on automated detection of complex legal language and misleading statements can also support enforcement, even if not explicitly framed as DP-related (e.g., Lippi et al., 2019). Backend detection capabilities need expansion as businesses may shift deceptions to invisible data flows — what Leiser & Santos (2023) call “Darkest Dark Patterns,” which enforcement agencies struggle with more than interface-level analysis.

R4

Establish Tech Adoption Pathways & Shared Spaces

What is lacking are shared spaces where different stakeholders can carry out common experimentation, and practical pathways to streamline the adoption of existing technology. This includes co-design processes, regulatory sandboxes, and open-source tools with appropriate licenses. Open-core licensing models — combining open source with proprietary features for specific clients — can bridge the incentive gap between academic openness and regulatory confidentiality.

R5

Balance Transparency with Confidentiality

The utmost need for confidentiality must be carefully balanced with the openness of scientific research. Concrete strategies include tiered access models, synthetic DP data for shared benchmarking, and publishing methodologies while protecting sensitive datasets. Opaque AI systems can only serve inspirational or exploratory functions in enforcement — never as direct evidence.

The incentive challenge. Tools that are needed in enforcement may attract fewer academic citations than trending topics. However, research assessment increasingly recognizes diverse forms of contribution (CoARA, 2022). We call for mechanisms that align incentives — from tailored funding instruments for joint academia-regulation objectives to R&D opportunities based on open-core licensing, and participation in public venues for intersectoral dialogue. All these activities would contribute to achieving digital fairness and augmenting the societal impact of scientific research.
